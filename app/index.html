Volcanic risk analysis as a tool for crisis management.

There is very limited public knowledge or political recognition of the potential hazards from Canadian volcanoes. This is due to their low level of activity and typically remote locations. They can, however, be sites for cascading hazards which can be exacerbated by changing climate.

One such system is the Mt. Meager Volcanic Complex. It is a dormant volcano 160 km NW of Vancouver, British Columbia, with >2 M years of geological history, recent explosive activity (eruption 2360 B.P.) and fumarolic degassing. Hydrothermal activity in conjunction with elevated rates of tectonic uplift have resulted in pervasive alteration and fracturing of the edifice to such an extent that Mt. Meager is the site of Canada’s largest recorded landslide (53 Mm3 in 2010). Satellite radar monitoring (InSAR) has detected over 27 active slopes with volumes > 0.5 Mm3 and one slope of potentially 300-500 Mm3 – the latter is moving at a likely unsustainable rate of 3.5 cm/month during late summer. A nearby hydro-electric facility could be directly impacted and there is a significant likelihood that, as in 2010, secondary flooding and debris will affect the village of Pemberton 60 km downstream.

Our Process

01.
Collaboration

Given the known risk, we have developed an informal public-private partnership with a broad range of academic, corporate and government partners.

02.
Observation and analysis

In the summer of 2019, we carried out extensive geophysical and geological mapping and are starting near- and real-time monitoring (e.g., satellite InSAR, seismic, infrasound, video, and gas chemistry) and development of an early warning system.

03.
Outcome

Mt. Meager will become the first volcano monitored in Canada and it is hoped that this initiative will assist in mitigating risk, building resiliency and supporting more effective response planning for the downstream communities.
